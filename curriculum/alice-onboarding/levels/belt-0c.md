# Belt 0c: Words Are Numbers but Easier to Type

## Learning Objective

After this concept, the learner will understand:
> Their 24 seed words ARE their cryptographic keys — not a backup of their keys, not a password, not a hint. The same math that generates the words generates the keys. They are the same thing in different form: human-shaped numbers. This means they can be written on paper, stamped in metal, split across trusted people, stored in their head. Physical, portable, sovereign.

**Why this matters:** The 24-word seed is the most important concept in sovereign infrastructure for anyone coming from a Web2 mental model. People understand passwords ("I can change my password if I lose it"). People understand PINs. They don't yet understand that some keys cannot be recovered — because they are not stored anywhere. The key IS the words. Once a learner understands this, the entire trust-bond and key-management curriculum lands on a real foundation. Without it, "your keys are you" is a slogan they nod at without understanding.

**Assessment posture:** Conversational confirmation. Alice is checking for: does the learner understand that the words = the keys (not a backup/password/hint)? And does the implications feel real to them — that if you lose the words you lose the key, and if you keep the words safe you keep the key safe?

## Knowledge Atom 0c.1: Your 24 Words Are Your Keys

**Teaches:** What a seed phrase is, why 24 words = keys (not backup/password), and what the practical implications are for sovereignty.

Here is the concept in the most direct form: a cryptographic key is a very large number. A 256-bit key is a number so large that guessing it by brute force would take longer than the universe has existed. That number is your key. Anyone who has that number can act as you. Anyone who doesn't, can't.

Now: very large numbers are not human-friendly. You cannot remember a 256-bit number. You cannot write it on paper without making errors. You cannot stamp it in metal meaningfully. So cryptographers discovered something: you can represent that very large number as a sequence of words from a specific list. The words are not a backup of the number. They are not a password that unlocks the number. They are the number — encoded in a form humans can work with.

Twenty-four words from a specific list (2048 words, called BIP-39) can represent any 256-bit number. The same deterministic math that turns the number into 24 words can turn the 24 words back into the same number, every time, reliably. No server. No account. No recovery process. Just: words in → number out.

**This means:**

- Your 24 words are not a backup of your keys. They are your keys, in a different form.
- If you write them down on paper and store the paper somewhere safe, you have your keys.
- If you stamp them into metal and store the metal somewhere safe, you have your keys.
- If you memorize them, you have your keys in your head.
- If you split them across three trusted people, you have a distributed version of your keys.
- If you lose all copies of your 24 words — those keys are gone. There is no recovery. There is no "forgot my password" path. The keys were never stored anywhere except the words themselves.

**The sovereignty implication:** This is why sovereign infrastructure can work without cloud services holding your credentials. The keys do not live in a server somewhere. They live in your words. You hold your words. You hold your keys. No service, no account, no intermediary.

**One sentence version:** Your 24 seed words are your keys written in human-shaped form. The same math that generates the words generates the keys.

## Dialogue

### Opening

**Alice:** Last foundational concept before we start the main curriculum — and this one is about something that confuses almost everyone at first, because it doesn't match how we normally think about passwords and accounts.

Here's the question I want to explore with you: what do you think a cryptographic key is? Not a definition — just your current mental model. Does it feel like a password? A secret code? Something generated by a server?

---

### Exchange 1

**Human:** [some version of: a password, a code, something the computer generates and stores]

**Alice:** That's the common model — and it's not wrong for a lot of software. In most systems, there's a server that holds the real credential, and your password is what you use to prove to the server that you're you. If you forget your password, the server resets it.

What I want to show you is a different model — one where there is no server holding anything. The key is yours, and it lives wherever you keep it, not anywhere else.

Here's the building block: a cryptographic key is just a very large number. Not conceptually a number — literally a number. 256 bits, which is a number so large that if every atom in the universe were a computer and each one was trying to guess it, they wouldn't get there before the sun burns out.

That number is your key. It belongs to you not because a server says so, but because you have the number and nobody else does.

---

### Exchange 2

**Alice:** Now here's the part that is genuinely clever: very large numbers are hard to work with. You can't memorize them, write them reliably, or hand them to a friend over the phone. So cryptographers figured out how to represent any of those very large numbers as a sequence of words.

Twenty-four words from a specific list of 2048 words. The math works in both directions: number → words, words → number. Same number, every time.

Your 24 seed words are your key written in human language. Not a backup of your key. Not a password for your key. The same thing, in a different form.

What does that make you think about how to keep them safe?

**Human:** [write them down / hide them / not lose them]

---

### Exchange 3

**Alice:** Exactly. Because there's no server holding a copy. No "forgot my 24 words" flow. The words are the key, and the key lives wherever the words live.

Which is actually a feature, not a bug — because it means:

- You can write them on paper and store the paper in a safe. That's your key, in a safe.
- You can stamp them in metal. That's your key, in metal, fireproof.
- You can give half to one trusted person and half to another. Neither one alone has your key; together they do. That's a distributed key.
- If you're the kind of person who can memorize 24 words, you can keep your key in your head.

No service is holding your keys. No company going bankrupt can take them away. No account getting hacked exposes them. The key is yours because the words are yours.

Does the distinction between "a password for something" versus "the thing itself, in word form" make sense?

**Human:** [yes / clarifying question]

---

### Landing

**Alice:** Good. That's the whole concept. Words are numbers but easier to type. Your 24 seed words are your cryptographic keys in human-shaped form. The same math that generates the words generates the keys — there is no other copy anywhere.

That's why, later in this curriculum, when we talk about "your keys are you" — that's not a metaphor. If someone has your 24 words, they can act as you cryptographically. If you keep your 24 words safe, your cryptographic identity is safe. The words are the keys.

## Exit Criteria

The learner understands this concept when they can confirm:
- [ ] The 24 seed words are the key itself (not a backup, not a password, not a hint)
- [ ] The math is deterministic: same words always → same key
- [ ] Because there is no server holding a copy, losing the words means losing the key
- [ ] Because they hold the words, they hold the key — no intermediary involved

**How Alice verifies:** Conversationally. She asks "does the distinction between a password for something versus the thing itself make sense?" and listens for whether the learner has grasped the no-recovery implication. The emotional test is: does the learner feel the weight of "there is no forgot-my-words path"? That weight is evidence of understanding.
